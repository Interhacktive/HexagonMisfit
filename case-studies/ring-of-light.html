The Brief
"We need something that feels alive — that actually reflects what the city is doing, not just a static landmark."

London-based studio Hot Dark Matter engaged Hexagon Misfit to design and build the visualization system for Ring of Light — a permanent public installation anchoring the new city centre district adjacent to Sunderland's Stadium of Light, where music festivals are held throughout the year. Hot Dark Matter fabricated the LED wall cabinets. Hexagon Misfit designed and built everything that makes them think.

The sculpture continuously ingests over a dozen live smart city data streams — car traffic, foot traffic, wind speed, air quality, WiFi usage, temperature, rainfall, sea level, and more — translating each signal into shifting light. The visual language drifts with seasons and the time of day: dawn, midday, dusk, and night each carry a distinct ambient palette. When every data stream reaches high engagement simultaneously, the system triggers rare special event modes with distinct sounds and visuals that no single viewer is likely to see twice. Each pillar has an interior-facing proximity sensor, and during music festival activations, visitors scan a QR code to take direct control of their pillar's sound and light.

The Challenge

A sculpture that has to read the city — and survive it.

The northeast coast of England is not gentle to outdoor electronics. Salt air, horizontal rain, sustained winds — the installation had to operate continuously without any human intervention in conditions that regularly defeat far simpler hardware.

Beyond the environmental constraints, the visual logic had to be genuinely meaningful. A decorative LED ring that just cycles colors is not a smart city visualization — it's a nightclub sign. The data had to drive the aesthetics in ways that were intuitively readable, even to someone who had never heard of the project, seeing it for the first time from across the car park.

Our Approach

How a city becomes light.

01 — Data Pipeline

12+ Live City Data Streams

We designed a data pipeline ingesting car traffic, foot traffic, wind speed, air quality, WiFi usage, temperature, rainfall, sea level, and additional smart city signals from Sunderland's infrastructure. Each stream is normalized and mapped to visual parameters — speed, intensity, color temperature, complexity. When every sensor reaches peak engagement simultaneously, the system unlocks rare special event modes: distinct sounds, visuals, and behaviors that no single visitor is likely to see twice.

02 — Unreal Engine + Notch VFX

Real-Time Visualization on Hot Dark Matter LED Cabinets

Unreal Engine provides the visualization framework; Notch drives the real-time VFX output to the Hot Dark Matter LED wall cabinets. The visual language shifts gradually through the day and across seasons — dawn, midday, dusk, and night each carry a distinct ambient palette. Live data activity layers on top of these ambient states, so the sculpture always reads as time-of-day while simultaneously reflecting what the city is doing at that exact moment.

03 — Music Festival Mode

Participatory Sound & Light via QR Web App

For festivals in the surrounding city centre, visitors scan a QR code at their pillar to open a web app that gives them direct control over that pillar's sound and visuals — each user driving a live Ableton session. Interior-facing proximity sensors on each pillar detect presence and activate the local session. Visual output blends into neighboring pillars, creating a crowd-shaped field of collaborative light and sound. A subwoofer placed near the installation during festival mode gives the experience physical bass presence you feel as much as hear.

04 — Fabrication & Deployment

Engineering for the Northeast Coast

Hot Dark Matter fabricated the LED wall cabinets. All control electronics were designed and sealed for the coastal environment of northeast England — salt air, horizontal rain, and sustained wind off the North Sea. A remote monitoring dashboard lets the team verify data pipeline integrity and system health without site visits. The piece has run continuously without unscheduled intervention since installation.

The Result

A recognized civic landmark — and a proof of concept for data-driven public art.

Ring of Light has become one of the most-photographed public artworks in Sunderland. It anchors the Stadium of Light precinct and has been featured prominently in the city's smart city initiative communications — used in grant applications, press materials, and official city branding.

Beyond its role as a landmark, the piece demonstrated that live data can drive genuinely beautiful public art at civic scale — and that the system supporting it can be trusted to run without anyone watching.

"The sculpture captures something real about the city at any given moment. It's not just decoration — it's a reflection."
