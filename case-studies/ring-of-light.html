The Brief
"We need something that feels alive — that actually reflects what the city is doing, not just a static landmark."

London-based studio Hot Dark Matter engaged Hexagon Misfit to design and build the visualization system for Ring of Light — a permanent public installation anchoring the new city centre district adjacent to Sunderland's Stadium of Light, where music festivals are held throughout the year. Hot Dark Matter fabricated the LED wall cabinets, made data drive audio and gathered sensor and city data. Hexagon Misfit designed and built everything that makes the pillar come to life visually.

The sculpture continuously ingests over a dozen live smart city data streams — car traffic, foot traffic, wind speed, air quality, WiFi usage, temperature, rainfall, sea level, and more — translating each signal into shifting light. The visual language drifts with seasons and the time of day: dawn, midday, dusk, and night each carry a distinct ambient palette. When every data stream reaches high engagement simultaneously, the system triggers rare special event modes with distinct sounds and visuals that no single viewer is likely to see twice. Each pillar has an interior-facing proximity sensor, and during music festival activations, visitors scan a QR code to take direct control of their pillar's sound and light.

The Challenge

Beautiful at 3am. Readable at noon. Controllable by a crowd of strangers.

The hardest design problem wasn't rendering 12+ live data streams — it was making them feel like a single coherent thing. Wind speed, rainfall, foot traffic, sea level, air quality, WiFi density, temperature, energy usage: each stream needed its own visual voice without any of them overpowering the others. Too literal and you get a dashboard. Too abstract and you get a screen saver. The aesthetic had to sit right in between — legible without labels, beautiful without being arbitrary.

That problem compounds across a full day. Dawn in Sunderland looks nothing like midday, and nothing like 2am on a Friday during a festival. The system needed to hold its own visual logic — a continuous, slow-shifting palette built from time of day and season — while layering live data activity on top without the whole thing feeling like it's fighting itself. The challenge was less "what does wind look like" and more "what does wind look like at dusk in October, with high foot traffic and low air quality, while still feeling like the same sculpture it was at noon."

During music festivals, a second layer of complexity arrives: up to a dozen people simultaneously controlling their own pillar's sound and light via QR web app. The interaction had to be immediately obvious — scan, see your pillar respond, understand you're driving it — without a word of instruction. And it had to stay harmonious. One person going full intensity can't wreck the experience for everyone else. The system needed soft limits, neighbor blending, and a visual language where individual contributions read as personal without fragmenting the whole ring into noise.

Our Approach

How a city becomes light.

01 — Data Pipeline

12+ Live City Data Streams

We designed a data pipeline ingesting car traffic, foot traffic, wind speed, air quality, WiFi usage, temperature, rainfall, sea level, and additional smart city signals from Sunderland's infrastructure. Each stream is normalized and mapped to visual parameters — speed, intensity, color temperature, complexity. When every sensor reaches peak engagement simultaneously, the system unlocks rare special event modes: distinct sounds, visuals, and behaviors that no single visitor is likely to see twice.

02 — Unreal Engine + Notch VFX

Real-Time Visualization on Hot Dark Matter LED Cabinets

Unreal Engine provides the visualization framework; Notch drives the real-time VFX output to the Hot Dark Matter LED wall cabinets. The visual language shifts gradually through the day and across seasons — dawn, midday, dusk, and night each carry a distinct ambient palette. Live data activity layers on top of these ambient states, so the sculpture always reads as time-of-day while simultaneously reflecting what the city is doing at that exact moment.

03 — Music Festival Mode

Participatory Sound & Light via QR Web App

For festivals in the surrounding city centre, visitors scan a QR code at their pillar to open a web app that gives them direct control over that pillar's sound and visuals — each user driving a live Ableton session. Interior-facing proximity sensors on each pillar detect presence and activate the local session. Visual output blends into neighboring pillars, creating a crowd-shaped field of collaborative light and sound. A subwoofer placed near the installation during festival mode gives the experience physical bass presence you feel as much as hear.

04 — Fabrication & Deployment

Engineering for the Northeast Coast

Hot Dark Matter fabricated the LED wall cabinets. All control electronics were designed and sealed for the coastal environment of northeast England — salt air, horizontal rain, and sustained wind off the North Sea. A remote monitoring dashboard lets the team verify data pipeline integrity and system health without site visits. The piece has run continuously without unscheduled intervention since installation.

The Result

A fixture outside the Stadium of Light — and a proof that city data can be genuinely beautiful.

For football fans gathering outside the Stadium of Light before and after a match, Ring of Light is part of the experience. The sculpture sits in their path, visibly alive — shifting with the weather, the time of day, the energy of the city around it. People stop. They look at it. They try to figure out what it's responding to. That curiosity is the piece working exactly as intended.

During festival activations, visitors discover they can control their pillar through the QR web app — and that moment of realization consistently draws people in. Watching your own input show up in the ring, then spread to the pillars beside you, tends to pull in nearby strangers who want to try it too.

Beyond the immediate experience, the piece demonstrates something that wasn't obvious before it existed: that smart city data — the dry infrastructure of traffic counts and air quality sensors — can be a material for public art. It runs continuously without anyone watching over it, and it looks different every time you see it.
