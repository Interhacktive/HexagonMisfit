The Brief
"In a world drowning in push notifications, we asked the obvious question: what if your message arrived by bird?"

Digital messages are weightless. They arrive in milliseconds and disappear just as fast — buried under a hundred more before you've had a chance to feel them. A handwritten letter, on the other hand, has gravity. It has a smell. It has the person's actual pen pressure. It was clearly worth the effort.

Pigeon bridges this gap with complete sincerity. You compose a note on your phone. We print it on paper, attach it to one of our certified city pigeons, and dispatch it to the recipient's window. No data plan required. No read receipts. No typing indicators. Just the soft thud of a bird landing and a small folded note that says what it says.

The brief was treated with the same earnestness we bring to any serious product. The logistics are absurd. The design is not.

The Problem

Modern messaging is too easy. That's the problem.

Sending a message today requires almost zero friction — and that frictionlessness has made messages feel disposable. When it costs nothing to send a thought, the thought costs nothing. The medium has eaten the meaning.

Email had a brief moment of formality before becoming a dumping ground. Texting elevated the casual to the dominant. Every subsequent platform has competed to make communication faster, lighter, cheaper. Nobody asked whether that was good.

Pigeon deliberately reintroduces friction. Not because we're nostalgists, but because friction is what makes something feel worth sending. A message carried by a real, living bird — a bird that someone trained, that has a name, that got tired halfway — that message means something. The design brief was to make the app feel as considered as the gesture itself.

Design Process

Four phases. One bird. Zero shortcuts.

01 — Research

Ornithological Constraints

We began where any responsible product team begins: with the hardware. Pigeons carry roughly 75g comfortably on a leg band. A standard Post-it note weighs 1g. This left significant design headroom. We also researched homing accuracy (within 1 mile in urban environments), average cruising speed (77 km/h), and the UX implications of a wet bird arriving at your fourth-floor window.

02 — Logo & Mark

The Pigeon as Wordmark

The logo brief: look like you mean it. We distilled a pigeon silhouette from photo reference into a clean geometric mark — rounded, confident, slightly puffed, as pigeons are. The gold (#C4A35A) was chosen for its warmth and its slight absurdity: this is a regal bird. The wordmark pairs Space Grotesk in medium weight with the mark, giving it the gravity of a financial institution and the plausibility of a startup.

03 — Color & Type

Warm, Aged, Slightly Official

The palette was built around the physical object being delivered: paper. Cream (#FAFAF8) as the base, aged gold (#C4A35A) as the primary action color, sage (#3D6B4F) as a supporting accent suggesting parks, rooftops, and the places pigeons tend to be. DM Sans for body copy — approachable, human. Space Grotesk for headings — just serious enough to be funny.

04 — Prototype

Three Screens, Full Flow

The app is three screens: Compose, Select Your Bird, and Track Delivery. Compose is a simple note editor with a paper texture and a word count limit (pigeons can only carry so much). Select Your Bird surfaces your assigned pigeon — name, breed, current mood estimate. Track Delivery shows a live map with a slowly, honestly moving pigeon icon. The entire flow was prototyped at full fidelity in Paper.design.

The Result

A complete product identity, designed to be taken completely seriously.

From the first logo sketch to the final prototype screen, Pigeon was designed as though it were real — because the only way a joke lands is if it's told straight. Every design decision was made earnestly: the color palette earns its warmth, the typography earns its authority, the interactions earn their restraint.

The result is a product that looks, moves, and feels like something you'd actually download. Whether you would actually use it to send a note via bird is, at this point, your business.

"Users reported feeling 'delighted,' 'genuinely charmed,' and 'mildly concerned about bird logistics.'"
